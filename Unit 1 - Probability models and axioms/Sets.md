A set us a collection of distinct elements, with some examples:
{a,b,c,d} -> finite
{$\mathbb{R}$} -> infinite
$x\in S$ -> "x belongs to set S"
$x\notin S$ -> "x doesn't belong to set S"
$\{ x\in \mathbb{R}: cos(x) \gt \frac{1}{2}$ -> We limit the set to where cos(x) is more than 1/2
$\Omega$ -> universal set
$S^c$ -> $x\in S^c$ if $x \in  \Omega$, $x \notin S$ and $(S^c)^c = S$ 
$\emptyset$ -> empty set
$\Omega^c = \emptyset$ 
$S \subset T$ -> $x \in S$ => $x\in T$ , aka S is a subset of T

### Unions and intersections
$S \cup T$ -> The union of two sets
$S \cap T$ -> The intersection of two sets
$S_n,\ n=1,2,...$ 

### Set properties
$$
S\cup T = T \cup S
$$
$$
S \cap (T\cup U) = (S\cap T) \cup (S \cap U)
$$
$$
(S^c)^c = S
$$
$$
S \cup \Omega = \Omega
$$
$$
S \cup (T \cup U) = (S \cup T) \cup U
$$
$$
S \cup (T \cap U) = (S\cup T) \cap (S \cup U)
$$
$$
S \cap S^c = \emptyset
$$
$$
S \cap \Omega = S
$$
### De morgan's law
$$
(S\cup T)^c = S^c\cap T^c
$$
$$
(\cup_n S_n)^c = \cap_n S_n^c
$$
